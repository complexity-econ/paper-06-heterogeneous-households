\begin{abstract}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

Papers~I--V of this series established that AI-driven automation in a
stock-flow consistent agent-based model (SFC-ABM) exhibits a reentrant
phase transition at a critical basic disposable payment
$\mathrm{BDP}_c \approx 500$~PLN, with mean-field universality invariant to
network topology, decision rules, and endogenous technology dynamics.
However, all prior work used an \emph{aggregate} household sector---a
single representative agent whose welfare metrics were computed post~hoc
from employment counts and wage totals. This paper replaces the aggregate
with 100,000 heterogeneous household agents, each endowed with individual
savings (log-normal, $\mu = 9.6$, $\sigma = 1.2$), debt (40\% indebted),
rent obligations ($\bar{r} = 1{,}800$ PLN), idiosyncratic marginal
propensity to consume (Beta-distributed, $\alpha = 8.2$, $\beta = 1.8$),
skill level subject to unemployment-driven decay, and health capital
subject to scarring.

Across five simulation campaigns (1,740 Monte Carlo runs, 30~seeds each),
we find three results invisible to the representative agent. First,
\textbf{aggregate metrics mask microstructural destruction}: the critical
point $\mathrm{BDP}_c = 500$ that appeared as a ``sweet spot'' in
Papers~I--IV (low Gini, moderate adoption) is in fact the point of
\emph{maximum} household bankruptcy (17.3\%), peak poverty (45\% at 50\%
median threshold), and peak income inequality (Gini $\approx 0.50$).
Second, \textbf{unemployment scarring creates a retraining catch-22}:
skill decay ($-2\%$/month) and health penalties ($+2\%$/month, cap~50\%)
accumulate during joblessness, reducing retraining success probability
precisely for those who need it most---even a doubled retraining
intensity yields only 18\% completion. Third, \textbf{UBI solves the
income problem but not the human capital problem}: high BDP ($\geq 3{,}000$
PLN) reduces income Gini to 0.15 and poverty below 10\%, but produces the
highest scarring levels as accelerated automation generates prolonged
unemployment spells.

These findings establish that the automation transition under UBI is not
merely a distributional challenge amenable to transfer policy, but a
\emph{structural} transformation whose costs are mediated by path-dependent
human capital destruction. The policy implication is that UBI requires
complementary interventions---particularly pre-emptive skill protection
before scarring onset---to prevent the emergence of a transfer-dependent
class excluded from productive employment.

\medskip\noindent
\textbf{Keywords:} agent-based model, stock-flow consistency,
heterogeneous households, universal basic income, automation, unemployment
scarring, bankruptcy cascades, retraining policy, inequality

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%======================================================================

The macroeconomic modeling of automation-driven labor market transitions
has relied predominantly on representative-agent frameworks, where a
single household sector aggregates consumption, savings, and labor supply
decisions \parencite{acemoglu2020, korinek2024}. This modeling choice
imposes a strong assumption: that the distributional consequences of
technological change can be inferred from aggregate employment and output
dynamics. The heterogeneous-agent New Keynesian (HANK) literature has
demonstrated that this assumption fails for monetary policy
\parencite{kaplan2018, krueger2016}; we show it fails equally for
automation policy.

Papers~I--V of this series constructed a stock-flow consistent
agent-based model (SFC-ABM) of AI-driven automation in the Polish
economy, progressively establishing: the acceleration paradox whereby
UBI catalyzes rather than cushions automation
\parencite{paperI}; the role of monetary regime in modulating
the transition \parencite{paperII}; empirical calibration of
sectoral elasticities \parencite{paperIII}; phase transition
universality across topologies and decision rules
\parencite{paperIV}; and robustness to endogenous technology and
network dynamics \parencite{paperV}. Throughout, the household
sector was a single aggregate: total wage income was computed as
$\text{employed} \times \text{wage}$, consumption as
$\text{income} \times \text{MPC}$, and welfare metrics (Gini,
unemployment rate) derived post~hoc from sector-level counts.

This paper replaces the aggregate household with 100,000 individual
agents, each with heterogeneous endowments, behavioral parameters, and
path-dependent state variables. The motivation is threefold. First, the
\emph{representative agent hides bankruptcy}: when aggregate income
falls, the representative agent reduces consumption smoothly, but
individual households with low savings and fixed obligations (rent, debt
service) face discrete bankruptcy events invisible to the aggregate.
Second, the \emph{aggregate conceals scarring}: unemployment in the
aggregate is a flow variable, but for individuals it is a duration that
progressively destroys skills and health---a mechanism emphasized in the
labor economics literature \parencite{arulampalam2001, jacobson1993,
blanchard1986} but absent from macro ABMs. Third, the \emph{aggregate
masks policy failures}: retraining programs appear effective in aggregate
(more programs $=$ more re-employment) but at the individual level,
scarring reduces the probability of successful retraining precisely for
those with the longest unemployment spells---a catch-22 that only
emerges with heterogeneous agents.

We organize the analysis around five simulation campaigns
(Table~\ref{tab:campaigns}): backward-compatibility validation (C1),
full BDP sweep with 21~levels (C2), MPC heterogeneity sensitivity (C3),
initial wealth distribution sensitivity (C4), and retraining policy
analysis (C5). The central finding is that the critical point
$\mathrm{BDP}_c = 500$ identified in Papers~I--IV as a ``sweet spot''
of high adoption and low aggregate inequality is, at the household level,
the point of maximum microstructural destruction.

\section{Model Extension: Heterogeneous Households}
\label{sec:model}
%======================================================================

We preserve the full SFC-ABM engine from Papers~I--V---10,000 firms
across six GUS-calibrated sectors, Watts-Strogatz inter-firm network
($k = 6$, $p = 0.10$), Taylor-rule monetary policy, and always-on
balance-sheet accounting \parencite{godley2007}---and replace the
aggregate household sector with individual agents. The extension is
backward-compatible: setting \texttt{HH\_MODE=aggregate} recovers the
Papers~I--V behavior exactly.

%----------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Household Agent}
\label{sec:household}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

Each household $i$ is a tuple
$(s_i, d_i, r_i, c_i, \sigma_i, h_i, \xi_i)$ where $s_i$ is savings,
$d_i$ is debt, $r_i$ is monthly rent, $c_i$ is the marginal propensity
to consume (MPC), $\sigma_i$ is skill level, $h_i$ is health penalty,
and $\xi_i$ is employment status. The status $\xi_i$ takes values in
\[
  \bigl\{\,\text{Employed}(f, k, w),\;
  \text{Unemployed}(m),\;
  \text{Retraining}(m, k, c),\;
  \text{Bankrupt}\,\bigr\}
\]
where $f$ is firm~ID, $k$ is sector, $w$ is wage, $m$ is months in
state, and $c$ is retraining cost.

\paragraph{Initialization.} Savings are drawn from
$\ln s_i \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_s, \sigma_s^2)$ with $\mu_s = 9.6$,
$\sigma_s = 1.2$ (median $\approx 15{,}000$ PLN, matching NBP 2023
household survey data). A fraction $\phi_d = 0.40$ of households carry
debt drawn from $\ln d_i \sim \mathcal{N}(10.5, 1.5^2)$ (median
$\approx 36{,}000$ PLN). Rent is drawn from
$r_i \sim \mathcal{N}(1{,}800, 400^2)$ with floor 800~PLN. MPC follows
$c_i \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)$ with $\alpha = 8.2$,
$\beta = 1.8$ (mean $\approx 0.82$, matching Polish household budget
survey propensities). Households are connected via a separate
Watts-Strogatz social network ($k = 6$, $p = 0.10$).

%----------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Monthly Household Step}
\label{sec:hh-step}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

Each month, for each household $i$:

\paragraph{1. Income.} Employed households receive wage $w_i$ plus
universal BDP. Unemployed households receive BDP only. Retraining
households receive $0.7 \times \text{BDP}$ (reduced availability).
Bankrupt households are absorbing---no further income or transitions.

\paragraph{2. Obligations.} Rent $r_i$ is deducted. Indebted households
pay debt service at rate $\delta = 2\%$/month of outstanding principal.

\paragraph{3. Consumption.} Disposable income (after obligations) is
consumed at rate $c_i$, split between domestic and imported goods using
the same import propensity as the aggregate model.

\paragraph{4. Bankruptcy.} If $s_i < -3 \times r_i$ (savings fall below
three months' negative rent), the household enters the absorbing
Bankrupt state.

\paragraph{5. Skill decay.} After an onset period of $\tau_0 = 3$ months
of unemployment, skills decay geometrically:
\begin{equation}
  \sigma_i(t+1) = (1 - \lambda_\sigma) \cdot \sigma_i(t),
  \quad \lambda_\sigma = 0.02 \text{ per month}
  \label{eq:skill-decay}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{6. Health scarring.} After the same onset period, a health
penalty accumulates linearly:
\begin{equation}
  h_i(t+1) = \min\bigl(h_{\max},\; h_i(t) + \lambda_h\bigr),
  \quad \lambda_h = 0.02, \; h_{\max} = 0.50
  \label{eq:health-scarring}
\end{equation}
The health penalty reduces consumption utility and retraining success
probability. The 3-month onset, 2\%/month rate, and 50\% cap are
calibrated to the scarring literature \parencite{arulampalam2001,
jacobson1993}.

\paragraph{7. Retraining.} Unemployed households with $m > 6$ months may
enter retraining with probability $p_r$ (baseline: 0.15) if savings
exceed the cost (baseline: 5,000~PLN). Social network effects add
$+0.05$ to $p_r$ if more than 30\% of neighbors are distressed.
Retraining lasts $\tau_r$ months (baseline: 6) after which success
probability is:
\begin{equation}
  p_{\text{success}} = p_0 \cdot \sigma_i \cdot (1 - h_i),
  \quad p_0 = 0.60
  \label{eq:retrain-success}
\end{equation}
This creates the catch-22: long-unemployed households have low $\sigma_i$
and high $h_i$, making retraining least likely to succeed for those who
need it most.

%----------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Labor Market}
\label{sec:labor}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

The individual labor market operates in three phases each month.
\emph{Separations}: workers at firms that automate or go bankrupt lose
employment. \emph{Job search}: unemployed households seek vacancies,
prioritizing their previous sector (sector-specific human capital), with
fallback to cross-sector matching ordered by sectoral CES elasticity
$\sigma_k$. \emph{Wage update}: employed workers' wages track the
macro-level market wage.

%----------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Social Network Effects}
\label{sec:social}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

The household social network (Watts-Strogatz, $k = 6$, $p = 0.10$)
transmits two behavioral signals. First, a \emph{precautionary saving
boost}: households with high neighbor distress ratios reduce consumption
(increase effective saving). Second, a \emph{retraining probability
boost}: concentrated neighborhood unemployment increases the likelihood
of entering retraining by 5~pp when the distress ratio exceeds 30\%.

%----------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{SFC Accounting}
\label{sec:sfc}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

All household flows (income, consumption, rent, debt service) are
integrated into the always-on balance-sheet check
\parencite{godley2007}. The SFC validator verifies three exact identities
each month: bank capital $=$ prior capital $-$ NPL loss $+$
$0.3 \times$ interest income $+ 0.3 \times$ household debt service;
bank deposits $=$ prior deposits $+$ total income $-$ total consumption;
government debt $=$ prior debt $+$ spending $-$ revenue. Any violation
$> 1.0$~PLN triggers a runtime assertion.

All campaigns use the PLN monetary regime with 10,000 firms across six
GUS-calibrated sectors and 100,000 individual households. Duration is
120~months with technology shock at month~30. Each seed uses an
independent random number generator for household initialization, firm
decisions, and labor market matching.

\paragraph{C1: Validation.} Five BDP levels (0, 500, 1000, 2000, 3000)
are run in both aggregate and individual mode. We verify that macro
aggregates (unemployment, adoption, inflation) match within 5\%,
confirming backward compatibility.

\paragraph{C2: BDP sweep.} Twenty-one BDP levels from 0 to 5,000~PLN in
250~PLN increments, each with 30~seeds. This is the core campaign,
generating the distributional analysis in Figures~1--4 and~6--8.

\paragraph{C3: MPC sensitivity.} Three Beta distributions: low variance
($\alpha = 16$), baseline ($\alpha = 8.2$), and high variance
($\alpha = 3$), tested at BDP $\in \{0, 500, 2000\}$.

\paragraph{C4: Wealth sensitivity.} Three initial savings distributions:
poor ($\mu_s = 8.5$, median $\approx 5{,}000$ PLN), baseline
($\mu_s = 9.6$, median $\approx 15{,}000$), and rich ($\mu_s = 10.5$,
median $\approx 36{,}000$), tested at the same three BDP levels.

\paragraph{C5: Retraining policy.} Three configurations: disabled
(no retraining), baseline ($p_r = 0.15$, cost $= 5{,}000$, duration
$= 6$ months), and enhanced ($p_r = 0.30$, cost $= 2{,}500$, duration
$= 3$ months), tested at three BDP levels.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}
%======================================================================

%----------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{The Critical Point Unmasked}
\label{sec:critical}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

Papers~I--IV identified $\mathrm{BDP}_c = 500$~PLN as the critical
point of the automation phase transition---the BDP level at which
adoption variance peaks and the system exhibits maximum sensitivity.
Aggregate metrics at this point (Gini $\approx 0.20$, moderate
unemployment) suggested a policy ``sweet spot.'' Individual household
data tells a different story.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{../figures/fig04_bankruptcy_cascade.png}
  \caption{Household bankruptcy rate at month~120 by BDP level (Campaign~2,
    30~seeds). The rate peaks at $\mathrm{BDP} = 500$ PLN (17.3\%), precisely
    at the critical point identified in Papers~I--IV. Error bars show
    $\pm 1\sigma$ across seeds.}
  \label{fig:bankruptcy}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:bankruptcy} shows that the bankruptcy rate peaks at
$\mathrm{BDP} = 500$ at 17.3\%---nearly one in five households is
financially destroyed. This is not a monotonic function of BDP: at
$\mathrm{BDP} = 0$, low automation preserves employment and keeps
bankruptcy around 5\%; at $\mathrm{BDP} \geq 3{,}000$, the transfer
itself prevents savings depletion. The critical point is the worst of
both worlds: automation accelerates (the acceleration paradox) but the
transfer is too small to cushion the resulting unemployment.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{../figures/fig03_gini_decomposition.png}
  \caption{Income Gini and wealth Gini at month~120 by BDP level
    (Campaign~2). Income Gini peaks at $\approx 0.50$ near
    $\mathrm{BDP}_c$, then falls as UBI provides an income floor. Wealth
    Gini remains flat at $\approx 0.14$ across all BDP levels---UBI
    redistributes income but not wealth.}
  \label{fig:gini}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:gini} decomposes inequality into income and wealth
components. Income Gini peaks at $\approx 0.50$ near the critical point
and declines monotonically with higher BDP, consistent with UBI's role
as an income floor. However, wealth Gini remains flat at $\approx 0.14$
regardless of BDP level. This decoupling---income inequality responsive
to policy, wealth inequality invariant---is invisible in the aggregate
model and has direct implications for long-run social mobility.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{../figures/fig06_welfare_comparison.png}
  \caption{Income Gini and poverty rate (50\% median threshold) by BDP
    level. Poverty is non-monotonic, peaking at $\approx 45\%$ for
    $\mathrm{BDP} = 500$--$1{,}000$ before declining at higher transfers.}
  \label{fig:welfare}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:welfare} confirms the non-monotonicity: the poverty rate
(fraction below 50\% of median income) peaks at 45\% in the critical
region and declines at higher BDP. This creates the paradox noted in
Section~\ref{sec:intro}: moderate UBI is \emph{worse} than no UBI on
poverty metrics, because it accelerates automation without providing
sufficient cushion.

\subsection{Unemployment Dynamics and Scarring}
\label{sec:scarring}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{../figures/fig01_distribution_evolution.png}
  \caption{Unemployment rate distribution over time by BDP level
    (Campaign~2). Violin widths show cross-seed variation. Unemployment
    rises monotonically from the technology shock (month~30), with higher
    BDP levels producing both higher mean unemployment and greater
    cross-seed dispersion.}
  \label{fig:unemployment}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{../figures/fig08_scarring_dynamics.png}
  \caption{Mean skill level and mean health penalty at month~120 by BDP
    level (Campaign~2). Both scarring channels worsen monotonically with
    BDP---higher automation creates longer unemployment spells, which
    degrade human capital through the mechanisms of
    Equations~(\ref{eq:skill-decay})--(\ref{eq:health-scarring}). Insets
    show the theoretical single-agent scarring curves.}
  \label{fig:scarring}
\end{figure}

Figures~\ref{fig:unemployment} and~\ref{fig:scarring} establish the
scarring channel. Higher BDP accelerates automation, which increases
both the level and duration of unemployment. After the 3-month onset
period, skill decay and health penalties accumulate monotonically. At
$\mathrm{BDP} = 5{,}000$, mean skill falls to approximately 55\% of
initial level and mean health penalty reaches $\approx 0.17$. This is
\emph{path-dependent}: the damage to human capital persists even if
employment is eventually restored.

%----------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Savings and the Buffer-Stock Mechanism}
\label{sec:savings}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{../figures/fig02_savings_trajectories.png}
  \caption{Terminal savings (mean and median) by BDP level (Campaign~2).
    Mean exceeds median at all BDP levels (right-skewed distribution).
    Both dip at $\mathrm{BDP} = 500$ where bankruptcy eliminates low-savings
    households, then rise as UBI supplements saving capacity.}
  \label{fig:savings}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:savings} shows terminal savings by BDP level. The
mean--median gap (right skewness) is persistent, consistent with
buffer-stock saving theory \parencite{carroll1997}. The dip at
$\mathrm{BDP} = 500$ reflects selective attrition: households with low
initial savings go bankrupt first, mechanically raising the median among
survivors. Above $\mathrm{BDP} = 1{,}000$, both mean and median rise as
the transfer supplements saving capacity.

%----------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Retraining: The Scarring Catch-22}
\label{sec:retraining}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{../figures/fig05_retraining_outcomes.png}
  \caption{Retraining funnel at $\mathrm{BDP} = 2{,}000$ PLN
    (Campaign~5). Enhanced retraining (doubled probability, halved cost
    and duration) generates $\approx 2\times$ more attempts but success
    rate remains $\approx 18\%$ in both regimes---because success depends
    on $\sigma_i \cdot (1 - h_i)$, which deteriorates during the
    unemployment spell preceding retraining entry.}
  \label{fig:retraining}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:retraining} presents the retraining funnel. The enhanced
policy doubles the retraining probability (0.30 vs 0.15) and halves both
cost (2,500 vs 5,000 PLN) and duration (3 vs 6 months). This generates
nearly twice as many attempts ($\approx 5{,}850$ vs $\approx 3{,}250$
per month). However, the success rate remains stubbornly around 18\% in
both regimes. The explanation lies in Equation~(\ref{eq:retrain-success}):
success probability depends on current skill and health, both of which
have deteriorated during the $> 6$ months of unemployment required before
retraining eligibility. This creates a structural catch-22: the
households that most need retraining have the lowest probability of
completing it successfully.

The policy implication is that retraining effectiveness depends
critically on \emph{timing}---intervening before scarring onset
($\tau_0 = 3$ months) would preserve the $p_0 = 0.60$ base success
rate. The current design, which requires 6~months of unemployment before
eligibility, guarantees that at least 3~months of scarring have already
occurred.

%----------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{MPC Heterogeneity}
\label{sec:mpc}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{../figures/fig07_mpc_sensitivity.png}
  \caption{Consumption distribution (P10--P90 band and median) across
    three MPC regimes at $\mathrm{BDP} = 2{,}000$ PLN (Campaign~3).
    The three regimes produce similar distributions, confirming that
    macro dynamics dominate micro consumption heterogeneity.}
  \label{fig:mpc}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:mpc} tests whether heterogeneity in the marginal
propensity to consume alters outcomes. Three Beta distributions---low
variance ($\alpha = 16$), baseline ($\alpha = 8.2$), and high variance
($\alpha = 3$)---produce similar P10--P90 consumption bands. This
robustness result is significant: it implies that the distributional
findings in Sections~\ref{sec:critical}--\ref{sec:retraining} are not
artifacts of the assumed MPC distribution. The macro dynamics (automation
decisions, labor market matching, BDP transfers) dominate the
micro-level consumption heterogeneity.

%======================================================================
\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%======================================================================

\subsection{The Aggregate Masks the Critical}

The central contribution of this paper is methodological: replacing the
representative agent with heterogeneous households reveals that the
aggregate welfare metrics used in Papers~I--IV gave a systematically
misleading picture of the critical point. Where aggregate Gini
$\approx 0.20$ at $\mathrm{BDP}_c = 500$ suggested moderate inequality,
individual-level Gini reaches 0.50. Where aggregate unemployment
$\approx 40\%$ is a single number, the household distribution shows
17.3\% bankruptcy, 45\% poverty, and severe scarring. This is not merely
a quantitative refinement---it reverses the policy interpretation of the
critical point from ``sweet spot'' to ``zone of maximum destruction.''

This finding resonates with the HANK literature
\parencite{kaplan2018}: heterogeneity is not a second-order correction
to aggregate dynamics but a first-order determinant of welfare outcomes.
The SFC-ABM framework, with its explicit tracking of every monetary
flow, is naturally suited to this disaggregation because balance-sheet
consistency is maintained at the individual level.

\subsection{Scarring as Hysteresis Mechanism}

The scarring mechanism (Equations~\ref{eq:skill-decay}--\ref{eq:health-scarring})
introduces genuine hysteresis into the model. Unlike Papers~I--V where
the automation transition was largely reversible (firms could re-hire),
human capital destruction is path-dependent. This connects to the
classical hysteresis literature \parencite{blanchard1986} and the
empirical scarring evidence \parencite{arulampalam2001, jacobson1993}:
unemployment duration has permanent effects on earnings, health, and
re-employment probability. The ABM makes this mechanism endogenous rather
than imposed through a reduced-form earnings penalty.

\subsection{The Retraining Paradox}

The catch-22 identified in Section~\ref{sec:retraining} has a direct
policy analog: active labor market programs (ALMPs) are empirically
most effective for the short-term unemployed and least effective for the
long-term unemployed \parencite{card2018, heckman1999}. Our model
provides a micro-founded explanation: it is not that programs are poorly
designed, but that the human capital base on which they operate has
already been eroded. The implication is that ALMP timing---intervening
before the scarring onset period---matters more than ALMP intensity.

\subsection{The Three-Way Policy Dilemma}

Combining the results yields a three-way dilemma with no UBI-only
solution:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{No UBI} ($\mathrm{BDP} = 0$): Low disruption, stable
    microstructure, but technological stagnation. The economy avoids the
    transition entirely.
  \item \textbf{Critical UBI} ($\mathrm{BDP} \approx 500$): Maximum
    disruption---peak bankruptcy, poverty, and inequality. The transfer
    catalyzes automation but is too small to cushion the consequences.
  \item \textbf{High UBI} ($\mathrm{BDP} \geq 3{,}000$): Low income
    inequality and poverty, but maximum scarring. Households are
    financially stable through transfers but permanently excluded from
    productive employment.
\end{enumerate}

None of these is unambiguously preferable. The choice depends on the
social welfare function: whether society values technological
modernization (favoring high BDP with complementary policies), stability
(favoring $\mathrm{BDP} = 0$), or some intermediate objective that the
UBI instrument alone cannot achieve.

\subsection{Limitations}

Several simplifications constrain the generality of these findings.
First, households do not optimize intertemporally---consumption follows
a fixed MPC rule rather than a consumption-saving problem. Second, the
labor market matching function is mechanical rather than search-theoretic.
Third, the scarring parameters ($\lambda_\sigma$, $\lambda_h$,
$\tau_0$) are calibrated to cross-country averages rather than Polish
micro-data. Fourth, the model does not include household formation,
dissolution, or intergenerational transfers. These are natural extensions
for future work.

%======================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%======================================================================

This paper demonstrates that replacing the representative household with
100,000 heterogeneous agents fundamentally changes the interpretation of
the SFC-ABM automation transition. The critical point
$\mathrm{BDP}_c = 500$~PLN, which appeared as a policy sweet spot in
aggregate analysis, is revealed as the zone of maximum microstructural
destruction: peak bankruptcy (17.3\%), peak poverty (45\%), and peak
income inequality (Gini $\approx 0.50$). Unemployment scarring---skill
decay and health penalties accumulating during joblessness---creates
path-dependent human capital destruction that undermines retraining
programs and generates a transfer-dependent class at high BDP levels.

The principal implication is that UBI addresses the \emph{income}
dimension of the automation transition but not the \emph{human capital}
dimension. Complementary policies---particularly pre-emptive skill
protection before scarring onset and early-intervention retraining---are
necessary to prevent the emergence of structural exclusion from
productive employment. The automation transition is not merely a
distributional problem amenable to transfers; it is a structural
transformation whose costs are mediated by path-dependent processes that
the representative agent, by construction, cannot capture.
